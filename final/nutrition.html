Sports Nutrition Guide

Key Point: You can't out-train a bad diet. Good nutrition is essential for athletic performance, and each player needs a personalized plan based on body size and position.

Energy Basics

Food energy is measured in calories (kcal). The three main energy sources (macronutrients) are:

Macronutrients and Energy
Macronutrient Energy (kcal per gram)
Carbohydrates4
Protein4
Fat9

Weight Management

  • Weight gain: Eat more calories than you burn
  • Weight loss: Eat fewer calories than you burn
  • Weight maintenance: Calories in = calories out

Carbohydrates

Main fuel for exercise. Two types:

  • Complex carbs: Slow energy release. Examples: whole grains, legumes.
  • Simple carbs: Quick energy. Examples: fruits, white bread. Good before/after training.

Protein

Repairs and builds muscle after exercise. Essential for recovery and growth. Found in meat, fish, eggs, beans.

Fats

Essential for health despite high calories. Needed for hormones and brain function. Sources: nuts, fish, oils, avocados.

Daily Intake Recommendations

  • Carbohydrates: 45–65% of total calories
  • Protein: 10–35% of total calories
  • Fat: 20–35% of total calories

Nutrient Timing Strategy

Pre-exercise (2 hours before): Light meal with all macronutrients. Example: oatmeal with fruit or chicken with sweet potato.

During exercise (over 1 hour): 30–60g carbs per hour, sports drinks or bananas every 15–20 minutes, water every 10–15 minutes.

Post-exercise (within 45 minutes): Mixed meal with carbs, protein, and fat. Example: pasta with meat and vegetables. Muscles best absorb nutrients at this time.

Important Notes

  • Micronutrients (vitamins/minerals) are essential.
  • Choose nutrient-dense foods over empty calories.
  • Strength coaches should refer complex nutrition issues to specialists.
  • Check anti-doping guidelines for any supplements.
